Labor Progression Prediction with Gaussian Processes

PeriGen / NIH / NICHD

This project reframed labor monitoring as a probabilistic forecasting problem: instead of comparing every patient to a single population curve, sparse Gaussian Process models updated individualized expectations and uncertainty as new cervical exams arrived.

What the model assumed

Let \(x_i\) denote time and observed maternal-fetal covariates at exam \(i\), and let \(y_i\) denote the corresponding labor state measurement. The modeling assumptions were:

  • observations arrive sequentially, so the forecast must update online rather than after a complete trajectory is known;
  • measurement noise is explicit, with \(y_i = f(x_i) + \varepsilon_i\) and \(\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)\);
  • the latent function \(f\) is smooth but patient-specific, which makes Gaussian Process priors attractive for partial trajectories.

That setup makes the page intentionally data-forward: the clinical value comes from how the method scales to tens of thousands of births while still returning individualized posterior forecasts.

Key outcomes

Results emphasized on this page

  • Large-scale validation on 49,694 births gave the forecasting approach clinical breadth.
  • Both cervical dilation and fetal station were modeled, rather than relying on a single summary signal.
  • Guideline curves from WHO and ACOG served as explicit comparators for practical relevance.
  • Uncertainty estimates were retained in the final output, not discarded after training.

Methodology Flow

1

Ingest longitudinal exams

Sequential cervical dilation, fetal station, and timing information define a partially observed labor trajectory for each patient.

2

Fit sparse GP structure

Sparse inducing-point approximations keep inference computationally practical on a dataset with nearly fifty thousand births.

3

Adapt to partial histories

Meta-learning components help the model respond quickly when only an early or mid-labor trajectory is available.

4

Compare against guidelines

Posterior forecasts are benchmarked against WHO and ACOG labor curves to quantify the value of individualized prediction.

Posterior mean

\[ \mu(x_*) = K(x_*, X)\left[K(X,X) + \sigma^2 I\right]^{-1} y \]

The posterior mean gives the expected future labor state for a candidate time-covariate point \(x_*\).

Posterior variance

\[ \operatorname{Var}(f_*) = K(x_*, x_*) - K(x_*, X)\left[K(X,X) + \sigma^2 I\right]^{-1}K(X, x_*) \]

The posterior variance quantifies how much confidence clinicians should place in the individualized forecast.
