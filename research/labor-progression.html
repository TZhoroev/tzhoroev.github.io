Labor Progression Prediction with Gaussian Processes

PeriGen / NIH / NICHD

Predicting labor progression is crucial for timely clinical decisions such as determining when to intervene with cesarean sections. Fixed-structure models like the Friedman curve assume labor follows a predefined pattern, fail to adapt in real time, and do not account for individual variations. This project developed a Sparse Gaussian Process model with meta-learning that provides patient-specific predictions with calibrated uncertainty.

Limitations of previous work

  • Rigid patterns: Models like the Friedman curve assume labor follows a predefined pattern, leading to inaccuracy when real data deviates.
  • No real-time adaptation: They do not update as new data becomes available, causing outdated predictions.
  • Population-based: Fail to account for individual variations common in labor.

Our approach

Using SGPR as a predictive model where each patient's data is treated as a separate meta-task. The model predicts two key factors: cervical dilation D(t) and fetal station S(t), using 6 input variables: D(t-1), effacement E(t-1), station S(t-1), contractions C(t-1), epidural use U, and labor induction L.

Key results

Conclusions

  • Developed a novel GP model that adapts to real-time labor progression with patient-specific data, providing both prediction and uncertainty.
  • Implemented meta-learning to allow the GP to learn from different patients and adapt quickly with minimal data.
  • Demonstrated superiority over static WHO and ACOG guidelines, potentially reducing unnecessary medical interventions.

Methodology

Model overview. The SGPR model takes 6 input variables from the previous time step and predicts cervical dilation D(t) and fetal station S(t). Each patient is treated as a separate meta-task.
Key innovations. SGPR introduces inducing points to reduce complexity from O(n³) to O(nm²). Meta-learning enables rapid adaptation to new patients with limited observations.
1

Ingest longitudinal exams

Sequential cervical dilation, fetal station, and timing information define a partially observed labor trajectory for each patient.

2

Fit sparse GP structure

Sparse inducing-point approximations keep inference computationally practical on a dataset with nearly fifty thousand births.

3

Adapt to partial histories

Meta-learning components help the model respond quickly when only an early or mid-labor trajectory is available.

4

Compare against guidelines

Posterior forecasts are benchmarked against WHO and ACOG labor curves to quantify the value of individualized prediction.
