Labor Progression Prediction with Gaussian Processes

PeriGen / NIH / NICHD

Sparse Gaussian Process models with meta-learning were developed to predict cervical dilation and fetal station in real time, turning large-scale obstetric data into individualized, uncertainty-aware labor forecasts.

Research Overview

This project focused on replacing one-size-fits-all labor curves with individualized probabilistic forecasts. By combining sparse Gaussian Process models with meta-learning, the system continuously updated predictions of cervical dilation and fetal station as new observations arrived during labor.

The models were validated on 49,694 births from the Consortium on Safe Labor dataset, providing a large and clinically meaningful test bed for real-time prediction. The emphasis was not only on point prediction, but also on calibrated uncertainty estimates that can support safer, context-aware decision making in obstetrics.

The resulting framework demonstrated stronger predictive performance than rule-based labor curves and thresholds from the World Health Organization (WHO) and the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG). Implementation was built with GPflow and TensorFlow, enabling scalable training and efficient deployment of sparse kernels in practice.

Core Contributions

  • Developed sparse Gaussian Process models with meta-learning to forecast labor progression at the patient level.
  • Modeled both cervical dilation and fetal station to support richer tracking of labor state over time.
  • Validated the framework on a large obstetric cohort of 49,694 births from the Consortium on Safe Labor.
  • Demonstrated superior predictive utility relative to WHO and ACOG labor curves and guideline-based thresholds.

Mathematical Formulation

Gaussian Processes provide a principled Bayesian framework for interpolation, forecasting, and uncertainty quantification. In this setting, posterior updates transformed partially observed labor trajectories into individualized predictive distributions for future labor states.

\[\mu(x_*) = K(x_*, X)\left[K(X, X) + \sigma^2 I\right]^{-1} y\]Posterior mean used to estimate the expected labor state at a future time or clinical covariate value.
\[\operatorname{Var}(f_*) = K(x_*, x_*) - K(x_*, X)\left[K(X, X) + \sigma^2 I\right]^{-1}K(X, x_*)\]Posterior variance used to quantify uncertainty, an essential ingredient for responsible real-time clinical decision support.

Why it matters

This work moves labor management away from rigid population averages and toward patient-specific probabilistic forecasting, allowing clinicians to reason about both expected progression and uncertainty in real time.

Methodology

Sparse Gaussian Process Modeling

Sparse inducing-point approximations preserved the expressive power of Gaussian Processes while keeping inference efficient enough for large-scale obstetric data and real-time updates.

Meta-Learned Trajectory Adaptation

Meta-learning strategies helped the model adapt rapidly to partial patient trajectories, making the framework more effective in early and mid-labor when observations are still limited.

Clinical Benchmarking

Model outputs were evaluated against canonical guideline curves, enabling a direct comparison between individualized probabilistic forecasts and standard labor management heuristics.
