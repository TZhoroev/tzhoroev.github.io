Predicting Alzheimer's Disease Progression

Johnson & Johnson · Summer Internship 2023

The objective was to predict the longitudinal progression of all 11 ADAS-Cog (Alzheimer's Disease Assessment Scale – Cognitive) subscores using Gaussian Process models with ordinal likelihood. ADAS-Cog is the gold-standard cognitive endpoint in AD clinical trials, and accurate subscore-level forecasting enables finer-grained understanding of how different cognitive domains decline over time.

Why subscore-level prediction?

Most AD progression models predict only the total ADAS-Cog score (0–70). However, cognitive decline is heterogeneous — memory, language, visuospatial, praxis, and attention domains deteriorate at different rates across patients. Predicting all 11 subscores individually allows clinicians and trial designers to:

  • Identify which cognitive domains decline fastest for a given patient
  • Detect early signals of treatment effect on specific cognitive functions
  • Design enrichment strategies for clinical trials based on domain-specific decline patterns

Modeling approach

Each ADAS-Cog subscore is modeled as a latent smooth function observed with uncertainty. Since subscores are ordered integers (not continuous values), a standard Gaussian likelihood is inappropriate. Instead, an ordinal likelihood maps the latent GP output through ordered thresholds to produce properly calibrated category probabilities.

Five GP configurations were systematically compared — from a continuous-response baseline to variational GP with ordinal likelihood — using 5-fold cross-validation to evaluate predictive accuracy and uncertainty calibration.

Ordinal Likelihood

Why ordinal?

ADAS-Cog subscores are ordered integers (e.g., Word Recall: 0–10). Treating them as continuous values ignores their discrete, ordered nature. The ordinal likelihood maps the latent GP function through ordered thresholds \(\tau_k\), producing calibrated probabilities for each score level.

\[ p(y_i = k \mid f_i) = g\big(\tau_k - f_i\big) - g\big(\tau_{k-1} - f_i\big) \]

where \(\tau_0 < \tau_1 < \cdots < \tau_K\) are learned thresholds and \(g\) is a monotonic link function (e.g., probit or logistic).

Key advantage

The ordinal model naturally encodes that a prediction of "score 5" should be more likely to err toward 4 or 6 than toward 0 or 10. This produces more clinically meaningful uncertainty estimates compared to Gaussian or classification likelihoods.

Combined with the GP prior's flexible longitudinal structure, this approach provides patient-specific progression curves with properly calibrated confidence intervals — essential for clinical trial planning and personalized treatment decisions.
